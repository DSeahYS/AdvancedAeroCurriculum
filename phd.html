Cosmology, Quantum Gravity, and Numerical Relativity.

FLRW Cosmology

The Friedmann-Lemaître-Robertson-Walker (FLRW) metric describes a homogeneous, isotropic, expanding universe. The dynamics of the scale factor $a(t)$ are governed by the Friedmann equations:

$$ H^2 = \left(\frac{\dot{a}}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{kc^2}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} $$ $$ \frac{\ddot{a}}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\left(\rho + \frac{3p}{c^2}\right) + \frac{\Lambda c^2}{3} $$

Current research focuses on the $\Lambda$CDM model, where Dark Energy ($\Lambda$) dominates the energy density of the universe today.

Quantum Field Theory in Curved Spacetime

At the PhD level, we study how quantum fields behave in non-Minkowskian backgrounds. A seminal result is Hawking Radiation.

Bogoliubov Transformations

The vacuum state is not invariant under general coordinate transformations. We relate creation and annihilation operators between two observers (e.g., at past null infinity and future null infinity) using Bogoliubov coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$:

$$ \hat{b}_j = \sum_i (\alpha_{ji}^* \hat{a}_i - \beta_{ji}^* \hat{a}_i^\dagger) $$

If $\beta_{ji} \neq 0$, the vacuum of one observer contains particles as seen by the other. For a black hole, this leads to a thermal spectrum with temperature:

$$ T_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B} $$

Numerical Relativity

Solving the Einstein Field Equations for complex systems (like binary black hole mergers) requires numerical simulations. The standard approach is the 3+1 decomposition (ADM Formalism).

The metric is decomposed into spatial metric $\gamma_{ij}$, lapse function $\alpha$, and shift vector $\beta^i$:

$$ ds^2 = -\alpha^2 dt^2 + \gamma_{ij}(dx^i + \beta^i dt)(dx^j + \beta^j dt) $$

This recasts the EFE into a Cauchy evolution problem, solvable on high-performance computing clusters.
